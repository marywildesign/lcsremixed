Title: longest collaborative sentence [remixed]
Editor: Mary G. Wilson

Acknowledgements

With thanks to the staff of the Whitney Museum of American Art for permission to work with Douglas Davis’ The World’s Longest Collaborative Sentence (1994–2012).

Notes

longest collaborative sentence [remixed] emerged from an MA Experiments course exploring ways of expanding Guttorm Nordø’s mail art publication El Djarida. The project shifts this investigation into the digital realm, using Douglas Davis’ internet-based collaborative artwork as a point of departure.

Davis’ sentence—open, anonymous, and unmoderated—offers an early and revealing example of digital freedom of expression. Its accumulation of voices exposes the tension between openness and harm, raising questions about where responsibility lies when collective authorship is left unchecked.

This website presents the original sentence as a continuously autoscrolling text, transforming it into a typographic environment rather than a fixed document. Multiple censorship modes allow readers to choose how the text is obscured, while never revealing the censored words themselves. In doing so, the project shifts the act of moderation from platform to user, creating a paradox in which censorship is both participatory and invisible.

Some methods of obscuring language are intentionally disruptive, introducing friction and visual noise, while others remain subtle and ambient. This spectrum reflects the varied approaches to moderation found in digital communities—from heavy-handed suppression to nuanced content management—and asks how each shapes participation, responsibility, and collective authorship.

Design & Implementation

The text autoscrolls to evoke continuity and flow, contrasting the finite, physical nature of mail art publications. Source material was collected via Python-based web scraping, conducted with permission, to preserve the integrity of the original work.

Censorship decisions were informed by close reading of the sentence, accounting not only for explicit language but also for subtler forms of aggression. Accessibility and reading patterns were considered throughout, particularly in the design of the obscuring methods.
